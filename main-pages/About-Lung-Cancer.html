LUNg CAncer

"Every breath is a reminder of the preciousness of life, and in the face of lung cancer, we stand resolute, fueling the fight with unwavering determination and the power of knowledge. Together, we strive for a future where every breath is free from the shadow of this disease."


Lung cancer is a complex and serious disease characterized by the uncontrolled growth of abnormal cells in the lungs. It is primarily caused by tobacco smoking, including both active smoking and exposure to secondhand smoke. However, other risk factors, such as exposure to asbestos, radon gas, air pollution, and genetic predisposition, can also contribute to its development. 

Lung cancer is categorized into two main types: non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) and small cell lung cancer (SCLC). NSCLC is the most common type, accounting for about 80-85% of cases, while SCLC represents approximately 10-15% of cases. Each type has distinct characteristics, treatment options, and prognoses.

TYPES OF LUNG CANCER

  • Non-Small Cell Lung Cancer (NSCLC)
  • Small Cell Lung Cancer (SCLC)

Non-Small Cell Lung Cancer (NSCLC)

  • Adenocarcinoma: This is the most common subtype of NSCLC, usually found in the outer areas of the lungs. It often develops in current or former smokers and can also affect non-smokers. Adenocarcinoma can spread to other parts of the body.
  • Squamous Cell Carcinoma: This type typically occurs in the central part of the lungs and is often associated with a history of smoking. It tends to grow slowly and is less likely to spread initially compared to other types.
  • Large Cell Carcinoma: This is a less common subtype of NSCLC. It can appear in any part of the lung and tends to grow and spread rapidly. It is often diagnosed when the cancer has already advanced.

Small Cell Lung Cancer (S CLC)

  • Small Cell Carcinoma (Oat Cell Cancer): This type of lung cancer is highly aggressive and tends to grow quickly. It is strongly associated with smoking and is often diagnosed at an advanced stage. Small cell carcinoma typically starts in the central part of the lungs and has a high tendency to spread to other organs.
  • Combined Small Cell Carcinoma: This subtype involves a mixture of small cell carcinoma and non-small cell carcinoma components, such as adenocarcinoma or squamous cell carcinoma.
  • Small Cell Carcinoma with Neuroendocrine Features: This subtype exhibits neuroendocrine features, including the production of hormones or neuropeptides, such as ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone) or ADH (antidiuretic hormone).
  • Small Cell Carcinoma with Variant Histology: This refers to SCLC that demonstrates atypical histological patterns, such as glandular or spindle cell differentiation, which may resemble other types of cancer.

Symptoms 

Lung cancer can present with various symptoms, although some individuals may not experience any symptoms in the early stages. Common symptoms of lung cancer include a persistent cough that worsens over time, coughing up blood or rust-colored sputum, shortness of breath, chest pain, wheezing, hoarseness, unexplained weight loss, fatigue, recurrent respiratory infections, swelling in the neck or face, and bone pain if the cancer has spread. It's important to note that these symptoms can also be caused by other conditions, but if you experience any of these symptoms and they persist or worsen, it's important to seek medical attention for a proper evaluation and diagnosis. Early detection of lung cancer can significantly improve treatment outcomes.

Diagnosis 

The diagnosis of lung cancer involves a comprehensive approach that includes a medical history assessment, physical examination, imaging tests, and tissue sampling. Doctors will review the patient's medical history and perform a physical examination to identify any signs or symptoms of lung cancer. Imaging tests like chest X-rays, CT scans, and PET scans are used to visualize the lungs and detect any abnormalities or tumor growth. Biopsy procedures, such as fine-needle aspiration, core biopsy, bronchoscopy, or mediastinoscopy, are performed to obtain tissue samples for microscopic examination and molecular testing. Molecular testing helps identify specific genetic mutations or abnormalities in the tumor that can guide personalized treatment decisions. Staging is then done to determine the extent of cancer spread, often with additional imaging tests. Early and accurate diagnosis, along with proper staging, is crucial for determining the most appropriate treatment options for lung cancer patients.

Treatment

Treatment options for lung cancer depend on the stage and type of the disease, as well as the overall health of the patient. They may include surgery to remove the tumor, radiation therapy to target and destroy cancer cells, chemotherapy to kill cancer cells throughout the body, targeted therapy to attack specific molecular changes in cancer cells, and immunotherapy to enhance the body's immune response against cancer.

Prevention

Prevention plays a crucial role in reducing the risk of lung cancer. Quitting smoking is the most effective measure one can take to prevent the disease. Additionally, minimizing exposure to environmental carcinogens, such as secondhand smoke, asbestos, and radon, can help lower the risk. Regular screenings with low-dose CT scans are recommended for individuals at high risk, as early detection can improve treatment outcomes.
